44 Zettabytes Yearly Healthcare generates enough data to fill 44 billion hard drives annually. The healthcare sector produces an estimated 44 zettabytes of data annually, driven by electronic health records, imaging, and genomic sequencing. To grasp this, imagine filling over 44 billion 1-terabyte hard drives, enough to store the entire internet multiple times.

Up to 2 Billion Genomes By 2025, 100M–2B human genomes may be sequenced, unlike the single genome of 2003. By 2025, projections suggest 100 million to 2 billion human genomes will have been sequenced, a massive leap from the single genome sequenced over 13 years by the Human Genome Project. This scale reflects advancements in high-throughput sequencing and AI-driven data processing.

5-Hour Sequencing A human genome can now be sequenced in just 5 hours and 2 minutes. In 2022, Stanford researchers set a record by sequencing a human genome in just 5 hours and 2 minutes using Oxford Nanopore’s PromethION and cloud-based computing. This is a dramatic improvement over the decade-plus required for the first genome, showcasing modern hardware and software capabilities.

8-Hour Analysis AI enables genome sequencing and analysis in under 8 hours for rapid diagnosis. AI and machine learning have slashed genome analysis time, enabling sequencing and interpretation in under 8 hours. This speed, achieved with tools like the DRAGEN platform, allows rapid diagnosis of genetic diseases, transforming clinical care.

$100 Genome Sequencing costs dropped from $3B to $100, a 30-million-fold reduction. The cost of sequencing a human genome has plummeted from $3 billion in 2003 to around $100 in 2025, a 30-million-fold decrease. This affordability, driven by technologies like Illumina’s NovaSeq X, makes genomic medicine accessible to millions.

20 Petabytes of Data The SRA holds data equivalent to 200M human genome sequences. The Sequence Read Archive (SRA) stores over 20 petabytes of sequencing data, equivalent to the raw data from sequencing over 200 million human genomes. This vast repository supports global research with cloud-based AI analysis.
